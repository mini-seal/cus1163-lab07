2. What would happen if the time quantum was 1ms? Would the system be more or less efficient? 

      If the time quantum were 1ms, the system would be less efficient because the CPU would spend much of its time 
      context switching rather than executing processes. This would lead to high overhead, increases average waiting time, 
      and reduce overall system performance.

3. What would happen if the time quantum was 20ms (larger than any process burst time)? How would Round Robin behave then? 
      
      If the time quantum were larger than any process's burst time, each process would finish execution without being preempted,
      and no process would be placed back into the Round Robin queue. So if we had a time quantum of 20ms, which is larger than any 
      process burst time, our Round Robin simulator wouldn't simulate Round Robin behavior because the circular loop it facilitates with 
      a queue would never occur.  It would behave like FCFS because the circular queue would never be used.

5. Why does Round Robin prevent the convoy effect that occurs in FCFS scheduling? 
      
      Round Robin prevents the convoy effect that occurs in FCFS by assigning a designated amount of time (time quantum) to each 
      process in the CPU; if it has not completed, it removes it and puts it back into the queue. This prevents short processes from 
      being stuck waiting behind long processes. All processes are sent to the CPU to be executed in parts, rather than waiting until
      a whole process is completed.
